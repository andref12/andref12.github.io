PCB Design Best Practices

Introduction


This is a collection of PCB layout best practices that I’ve put together to help during the design process and help improve existing projects. If you notice anything incorrect or missing, feel free to reach out or open an issue on the repository.

Read The Docs

The datasheets from the components you are going to use contain a lot of valuable information that will help guide your design.

Placement

Decoupling Capacitors

As close to IC pin as possible. In case there is multiple capacitors the ones with the lowest value should be the closest to the pin.

Crystal

As close to the microcontroller pin as possible.

ESD protection

As close to the connector/component as possible.

Mounting Holes

Due to stress that maybe caused during the mounting process keep traces and components as far away as possible from the mounting holes.

Height and Weight

Components or modules heavier than 7g and or bigger than 450m2 must be keept on the top layer to avoid the need of glue or possible process failures.

Stencil

Avoid placing components with very different pad sizes close to each other due to the variability in the recommended stencil thickness. This can cause stress during manufacturing and also reduce the stencil’s lifespan.

Vias

Placing pads near unfilled vias can cause a defect known as solder wicking, where the solder that should remain on the pad flows down the via. This may result in poor solder joints on the component and the formation of a solder bump on the opposite side of the board, which can damage the stencil.

Routing

Clock Signal

The clock signal is considered a agressor and is recomended to trace it keeping a 3W distance from other traces with W being the width of the clock trace.

Power/VCC

Make sure the track and vias supports the amount of current required by the component being routed.

High Speed Signals

When changing layers there should be a GND via next to the Signal via so that the return current has a path to flow to.

Copper Between Component Pads

Do not route traces directly between adjacent component pads on outer PCB layers.

Do not place copper pours/planes between adjacent component pads on outer PCB layers.

This can look like a solder bridge during a PCB inspection and when trying to repair the PCB may be damaged.

This suggestion may be overriden to comply with electrial current capacity, thermal dissipation and RF requirements.

Thermal Reliefs

Thermal Reliefs for SMD

Thermal relief is a technique used in PCB manufacturing to prevent tombstoning defects.

For 0805 and smaller discrete components ensure the cumulative width of traces attached to the pads does not exceed a 3 to 1 ratio between pads.

Thermal Reliefs for THMT

This suggestion is mostly for tin lead and lead-free wave and selective soldering. Not complying can cause insufficient hole fill, loss of solder fillet and rework issues.

A maximum of two inner layer planes cam be connected to a single PTH barrel

Provide a maximum of four thermal relief spokes per barrel per layer.

The maximum relief spoke width 0.25mm (A)

The minimum relief spoke length 0.25mm (B)

Through Hole Components (PTH)

  • Oblong holes should be used to accommodate components with high tolerance in lead spacing.
  • To maintain a uniform mounting height for PTH components and prevent solder meniscus issues, the Kink (Standoff Bend) technique is recommended.
  • Avoid excessively large holes, as they may cause soldering defects and solder balls.

Fiducial Marker

PCB panel fiducial point assists in the calibration of the pick-and-place machine and other tooling.

The ideal positioning is at the farthest points along the largest diagonal of the panel.

Solder Mask Defined x Non Solder Mask Defined

In a Non-Solder Mask Defined (NSMD) Pad the solder mask opening is larger than the copper pad and the copper pad itself defines the solderable area. The advantages are a better solder joint reliability (solder can wrap around pad edges) and more forgiving to mask alignment tolerances. One of its disadvantages is that its slightly weaker mechanically (pad can lift more easily if stressed).

In the case of a Solder Mask Defined (SMD) Pad the solder mask opening is smaller than the copper pad and overlaps the pad edges, defining its size.

Advantages:

  • Stronger mechanical support against pad lifting.

Disadvantages:

  • Reduced solderable surface → can lead to weaker solder joints.
  • The solder mask can interfere with the components height which can cause misalignment and other problems in the smd process.

RF Design

To ensure RF performance and reliability, follow the RF layout design principles below:

  • Use an impedance simulation tool to accurately control the characteristic impedance of RF traces to 50 Ω.
  • Ground pads adjacent to RF pins should not use thermal reliefs, they must be fully connected to the ground plane.
  • Keep the distance between RF pins and the RF connector as short as possible, and avoid right-angle trace corners. Use curved traces instead, a 135° trace angle is recommended.
  • Ensure there is clearance beneath the signal pin of the antenna connector or solder joint.
  • The reference ground for RF traces should be continuous. Adding ground vias around RF traces and along the ground reference can improve RF performance. The distance between ground vias and RF traces should be at least twice the RF trace width (2 × W).
  • Keep RF traces away from interference sources such as DC-DC converters, high-frequency digital lines, display interfaces, and clock signals. Avoid intersections or parallel routing with other traces on adjacent layers.

Identification

If possible leave space for a serial number or barcode to enable a way to identify each individual PCB.

Possible identification methods:

  • Laser Engraving
  • Adheseve tag

Design For Testability (DFT)

Bed of Nails

  • Provide test points for each net, or for as many nets as possible.
  • Provide at least 3 tooling holes with 3.175mm
  • Avoid placing testpoints close to the boards edge
  • The recommended spacing between test point centers is 100 mils, with a minimum spacing of 50 mils due to bed-of-nails fixture limitations.
  • The preferred layer for placing test points is one without PTH components, or with the lowest possible number of them.
  • It is recommended that test points be placed at least 3 mm away from PTH solder joints.

Flying Probe

  • Use pads that will leave a bit of exposed copper beyond component and solder if possible
